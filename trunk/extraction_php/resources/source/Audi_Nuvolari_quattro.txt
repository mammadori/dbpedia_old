The '''Audi Nuvolari quattro''' (also known as the '''Audi Nuvolari''') was a [[concept car]] created by [[Germany|German]] automobile maker [[Audi]].  This vehicle was first introduced at the 2003 [[Geneva Motor Show]].  It was what Audi planned to become the vision of their next [[grand tourer]] (GT) vehicle.  It was the second of three concept cars designed by [[Audi]] and shown in 2003, after the [[Audi Pikes Peak quattro|Pikes Peak quattro]] and ahead of the [[Audi Le Mans quattro|Le Mans quattro]].<ref>{{PDF|[http://www.audi.com/etc/medialib/cms4imp/audi2/company/financial_information/pdf.Par.0103.File.pdf Audi 2003 Product Strategy - indicating Audi Nuvolari quattro detail (p.13)]|1.50&nbsp;MB}} - </ref><ref>[http://www.conceptcarz.com/vehicle/z6932/Audi_Nuvolari.aspx Audi Nuvolari quattro study, from ConceptCarz.com]</ref>

The Nuvolari quattro had a 5.0&nbsp;L [[twin-turbo]]charged [[V10 engine|V10]] [[gasoline direct injection|Fuel Stratified Injection]] (FSI) engine rated at {{convert|441|kW|PS bhp|0|lk=on|abbr=on}}.  [[Torque]] on the Nuvolari quattro was rated at {{convert|750|Nm|0|lk=on|abbr=on}}.  The Nuvolari quattro used Audi's [[Torsen]]-based [[quattro (four wheel drive system)|quattro]] permanent [[four-wheel drive]] system.

It could accelerate from 0 to {{convert|100|km/h|mph||abbr=on}} in about 4.1 seconds.  Top speed was electronically governed to {{convert|250|km/h|mph||abbr=on}}.

The Nuvolari quattro was named after one of the most famous and legendary race car drivers of all time, [[Tazio Nuvolari]].  Nuvolari gained 61 [[Grand Prix motor racing|Grand Prix]] victories and died in 1953.  Fifty years after his death, the Audi Nuvolari quattro was created in honour of his memory.

Audi subsequently developed the Nuvolari quattro into the new [[Audi A5|A5 and S5]] range.
