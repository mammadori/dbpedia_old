The compression-ignition [[diesel engine]]s listed below are currently being installed by various [[marque]]s of [[automobile]]s and [[commercial vehicle]]s, along with [[marine engine|Marine Motor]]<ref name=VW_Marine>{{cite web|url=http://www.vw-m.de/index.php?id=6&L=1|title=Volkswagen Marine > Engines <|work=[[Volkswagen Group]]|publisher=Volkswagen.de|year=2009|accessdate=4 November 2009}}</ref> and [[Volkswagen Industrial Motor]]<ref>{{citation|url=http://www.mi-uk.com/products/engines/downloads/sdi19industrial.pdf|title=The SDI 1.9 Industrial Engine|format=PDF|work=[[Volkswagen Group|Volkswagen AG]]|publisher=mi-uk.com|date=March 2005|accessdate=4 November 2009}}</ref> applications, of the [[Germany|German]] [[automotive industry]] conglomerate and holding company - [[Volkswagen Group]] (VWAG).  All [[list of Volkswagen engines|listed engines]] operate on the [[four-stroke engine|four-stroke cycle]], and unless stated otherwise, use a [[wet sump]] [[motor oil|lubrication]] system, and are [[water cooling|water cooled]].

Since the Volkswagen Group is [[Europe]]an, official [[internal combustion engine]] performance ratings are published using the [[International System of Units]] (commonly abbreviated "SI"), a modern form of the [[metric system]] of figures.  [[Motor vehicle]] engines will have been tested by a [[Deutsches Institut für Normung]] (DIN) accredited testing facility, to either the original ''80/1269/[[European Economic Community|EEC]]'', or the later ''1999/99/[[EC]]'' standards.  The standard initial measuring unit for establishing the rated [[motive power|motive]] [[power (physics)|power]] output is the [[watt#kilowatt|kilowatt]] (kW); and in their official literature, the power rating may be published in either the kW, or the [[horsepower#PS|'Pferdestärke']] (PS), or both, and may also include conversions to imperial units such as the [[horsepower]] (hp) or [[horsepower#brake horsepower|brake horsepower]] (bhp).  ([[conversion of units#power or heat flow rate|Conversions]]: one&nbsp;PS ≈ 735.5&nbsp;[[watt]]s (W), ≈ 0.98632&nbsp;hp (SAE)).  (The PS figure is sometimes incorrectly referred to as 'metric horsepower').  In case of conflict, the metric power figure of kilowatts (kW) will be stated as the primary figure of reference.  For the turning force generated by the engine, the [[Newton metre]] (Nm) will be the reference figure of [[torque]].  Furthermore, in accordance with European automotive traditions, engines shall be listed in the following ''ascending'' order of preference:
#Number of [[cylinder (engine)|cylinders]],
#[[Engine displacement]] (in [[litre]]s),
#[[Engine configuration]], and
#Rated [[motive power|motive]] [[power (physics)|power]] output (in [[watt#kilowatt|kilowatts]]).

As with the discontinued Volkswagen Group diesel engines, whilst the [[Volkswagen Golf]] may have started the diesel craze in Europe, every diesel engine in each generation of the Volkswagen Golf has been offered in Industrial applications, and has since had impressive field results on par with a [[Isuzu]], [[Kubota]], or [[Yanmar]] Industrial diesel engine in industrial applications, and has been as much of a workhorse as the latter Japanese choices.

==Three & four cylinder EA111 diesels{{anchor|EA111}}==
The '''EA111''' series of [[internal combustion engine|internal combustion]] [[list of Volkswagen engines|engines]] was introduced in the mid [[1970s]] in the [[Audi 50]], and shortly after in the original [[Volkswagen Polo]].  It is a series of [[water cooling|water cooled]] [[straight-three engine|inline three-]] and [[inline-four engine|inline four-cylinder]] [[petrol engine|petrol]] and diesel engines, in a variety of [[engine displacement|displacement sizes]].  This [[overhead camshaft]] engine features a [[crossflow cylinder head]] design, and directly-driven auxiliary units.{{clarify|date=June 2009}}  The exhaust side is in driving direction, closest to the front of the vehicle.
